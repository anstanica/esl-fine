jobs | Lesson Plan

What is a job?
A job is a paid position of regular employment.
Why you must teach jobs to your students?
Jobs are a part of our daily lives. Teaching jobs to your students will help them to understand the importance of jobs in our daily lives.
- Jobs like : - doctor, teacher, engineer, lawyer, police officer, firefighter, nurse, pilot, astronaut, chef, farmer, etc.

Introduction

- To introduce the lesson, ask your students to tell you what they know about jobs.
- Show them the flashcards and ask them to tell you what they see, It's completely fine if they start talking in their native language. Actually, it's a good thing, give them time to think and discuss the topic in their native language.
- Then ask them to tell you what they see in English.
- Elicit the answers from your students, and let the students who got the answers right to come to the front of the class and point to the flashcards, and repeat the word.
- If they have already learned the Family lesson, review the family members with them.
- Finally, you can start the lesson by saying "Today we are going to learn about jobs". Let them repeat after you. "Jobs", "Jobs"

Practice

To practice the jobs, we can use the flashcards . Next show them Flashcards one by one and act it out. Ask the students to do the same.
Then use play pass the flashcards. The students will pass the flashcards to each other and say the word.

  • Isolation practice : isolation practice as mentioned in the activity
    above (Asking and practicing one by one).

  • Pair practice : where the students will work in pairs. example:
    Student A: what do you?
    Student B: I am a doctor.

  • Group practice : as the name suggests is where the students will work in groups. group games , group activities, and group points.

    • You can apply the practice in any order you want.
    But we recommend that you start with the isolation practice, then the pair practice, and finally the group practice.

How to explain a game or an activity to ESL Students

  • PPT Games

    • Car Race



      - Divide class into teams
      - Each team is asked a question if they answer correctly click there teams number button to move their car.
      - Five correct answers will cross the finish line and win the race!

    • WhaT is Missing?

       

      How to play what is missing game?

      1. Divide the class into two teams.
      2. Show the first slide and ask the students to look at the pictures for 60 seconds.
      3. Then move to the next slide and ask the students to find the missing word.
      4. Pick a student from each team to answer.
      5. The first team to find the missing word wins a point.
      6. The team with the most points wins the game.

    • Zombie hunt jobs & workplaces

       

      - Zombie hunt to practice jobs and workplaces vocabulary.
      - Shoot the zombies with the correct answer.
      - If you shoot the wrong zombie it will give you a scary laugh.

  • In-Class Games

    • Charades

      How to play charades:
      - Write the names of jobs on pieces of paper. Or you can use small cards   with the names of the jobs "included in the ppt".
      - Put the pieces of paper in a hat / bag.
      - You can divide the class into two teams.
      - Have the students take turns drawing a piece of paper from the hat.
      - The students then act out the job on the paper.
      - The other students try to guess the job.
      - The students who guess the job correctly get a point.
      - The team with the most points at the end of the game wins.

    • What Do You Want to Be?

      this game is a great way to get your students talking about their future.

      Play this game in pairs:
      - Prepare a set of cards.
      - The students take turns drawing a card from the pile.
      - The first student asks the second student "What do you want to be?"
      - The second student answers "I want to be a (doctor)."
      - The first student then asks "Why do you want to be a (doctor)?"
      - The second student answers "Because I want to help people."
      - Then the first student draw a new card and the game continues.

    • Join the Dots


      - Draw a five-by-five dotted grid on the board.
      - Divide the class into teams, and give each team a different number.
      - Take it in turns to ask each team a quiz question.
      - If a team answers correctly, they connect two of the dots with a line     either horizontally or vertically.
      - The aim of the game is to join the dots together to make squares. When   a team forms a square as a result of drawing a fourth line, they write   their team's number in the square and have another turn.
      - The game is over when all 16 squares have been completed. The team with the most squares wins the game.

  • Online Games.

    • Jobs



      here are 2 parts. In the first part, you have to match the images to the jobs. For example, you match the word "farmer" to the image of the farmer. In the second part, you have to look at the silhouette and guess which job it is. Then, you have to complete the sentence to describe what the job does. There are 104 jobs in total, and these cover every job that I could think to represent visually, This game took a lot of time and effort, so I hope you can enjoy and make good use of it.

    • Monster Vocab

      - This is a simple game for practicing basic vocabulary.
      - To play, you click on the monster's head to make it say the word.
      - Then you click on the correct picture to match the word.
      - If you get it right, the monster will say "Yes!" and the picture will turn green.
      - If you get it wrong, the monster will say "No!" and the picture will turn red.
      - You can click on the monster's head again to hear the word again.

    • Describer



      - This is a simple game for practicing basic reading comprehension. To play, you read the brief description and then click on the image that it best describes.

Wrap up / Review

To wrap-up up the jobs lesson, you can do the following:
Ask the students "What do you want to be when you grow up?"
As every student answers, write their answer and their name on the board.
Then, Call them by their title / job , Example "Doctor, Mark" , "Teacher, John"
Use their title / job to ask them questions, to greet them, to say goodbye to them, etc.
