Alphabets| Lesson Plan

alphabets are the basic building blocks of the English language. They are the core of writing and reading, that's why it is important to teach them to children at an early age. This lesson plan will help you teach alphabets to your students.
Remember we use TEFL methodology to teach alphabets to students

Presentation, Practice and Production.

Presentation : where the teacher introduces the topic to the students.
Practice : where the teacher gives the students exercises to practice the topic.
Production : where the teacher gives the students a chance to use the topic in a real life situation.

Warm Up

We will start this the warmup activity with a song that majority of the students are familiar with.
Alphabets song

How to teach a song



Introduction

Start by telling the students what are the alphabets and what they are used for.
Show them a word they are familiar with and spell it, as an example.
Example
Word : cat
Spelling : c-a-t

Click on the flashcards to preview before download.



Practice

First : Show the students the flashcards and ask them to repeat the alphabets after you.
Second : While the students are repeating the alphabets, do the alphabets shapes with your hands, or your body.
For example, if the letter is A you can do the shape of the letter with your body: legs apart, arms stretched out, and then bring them together.

  • Isolation practice : isolation practice as mentioned in the activity above (Asking and practicing one by one).

  • Pair practice : where the students will work in pairs. example:
    Student A: what is this?
    Student B: It's a pencil.

  • Group practice : as the name suggests is where the students will work in groups. group games , group activities, and group points.

You can apply the practice in any order you want.
But we recommend that you start with the isolation practice, then the pair practice, and finally the group practice.

Below are some activities for practicing this lesson:

Games


How to explain a game or an activity to ESL Students

  • PPT Games

    • Swimming Letters



      Swimming to the letters and click pop the balloons.

    • ABC Matching

       

      Easy and fun Abc matching game.
      Choose the first letter that matches the picture.

  • In-Class Games

    • Mystery Bag


      This is one of the easiest alphabet games for kids and doesn’t require much preparation.
      Using a bag, put objects inside such as (button, pen, apple ect), or you can put the letters.
      Children will reach into the bag and pull out the object / the letter, they will have to guess what letter the object starts with and say the sound of the letter.

    • Hidden Letter


      to play this game is by having objects spread out on the floor, say the name of an object and the students have to find the hidden letter and match it to the correct object.

    • Alphabet Ball

      Use a beach ball and write letters all over it, have the students make a circle and throw it to one another. When a student catches the ball, they have to say whatever letter their finger lands on and give an example of the letter. To make the game more challenging students that catch the ball will say the letter and then throw it to a following student with the first name of that letter.

    • Big and Small Race

      Place capital and lowercase letters all around the room. Have two students come up and when the teacher says start the students have to find all the capital and lowercase letters in the room and place them in order on the floor. Whichever student does it the fastest wins.

    • Memory Match

      Simply write each capital letter on one card and its lower case letter on the other card.
      Turn all the cards over and arrange them on a desk. Students must find both the capital and lowercase letter to make a match.
      Or make the game more challenging for your students and add a vocabulary element. Have students match an alphabet letter to a picture that starts with that letter

    • Alphabet Hop Scotch

      Get a foam alphabet puzzle and assemble it in a large space in your classroom.
      Put letters in random order, but limit the layout of the puzzle to four across.
      Students then take turns hopping through the alphabet board. Have students either name the letter, its sound or a word that begins with that letter before hopping on to a given square.
      Students can choose which squares they step on, but their goal is to work from one end of the puzzle to the other end. Give each student at least one turn hopping through the alphabet.

Wrap up / Review

Wrap this lesson up by having students spelling their names using the letters they learned today.
For young learners, you can have them cut out the letters and glue them in the correct order.
